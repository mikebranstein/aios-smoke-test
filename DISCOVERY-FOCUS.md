# DISCOVERY-FOCUS

### 1. Product Scope & Mission

- Define an uplifting movement-rhythm PC game centered on jump-rope performance, combining precision timing and creative expression.
- Deliver an experience that is approachable on first play, deep at mastery, and rewarding across solo and co-op play.
- Translate player performance into reactive spectacle (audio, visuals, crowd/world feedback) to reinforce momentum and identity.

### 2. Target Users

- Rhythm-action players who want tight timing control and score optimization.
- Creative expression players who want to build a recognizable personal style through tricks, routing, and cosmetics.
- Mastery-driven players who value long-term progression, higher difficulty ceilings, and leaderboard improvement.
- Social-but-flexible players who want co-op synchronization and asynchronous rivalry without requiring always-on competitive play. (inferred)

### 3. In Scope / Out of Scope

**In Scope**
- PC-first release target.
- Single-player and co-op core gameplay.
- Modes explicitly named in foundation: Story Tour, Freestyle Studio, Time Trials, Co-op Sync, Rival Arena.
- Progression via skill specialization, reputation growth, and style personalization.

**Out of Scope**
- Non-PC platform launch commitments.
- Full-contact sports simulation or realism-first athletics framing. (inferred)
- Non-rhythm core genres that remove timing/flow mastery as the central gameplay loop. (inferred)

### 4. Technical Constraints

- Platform constraint: ideas must ship on PC.
- Core input loop must preserve readable timing windows and low-friction control responsiveness.
- Systems must support performance-reactive presentation (dynamic music layering, stage/crowd feedback) without compromising gameplay readability.
- Multiplayer concepts must fit a two-player synchronized co-op mode plus asynchronous ranking/duel structures.

### 5. Priority Problems

- Helping first-session players reliably achieve an early “clean run” so onboarding feels encouraging, not punishing. (inferred)
- Balancing accessibility with a high mastery ceiling in timing, trick grammar, and combo routing. (inferred)
- Ensuring scoring fairly rewards both technical consistency and expressive style without becoming opaque. (inferred)
- Keeping progression loops motivating across beginner, intermediate, and expert cohorts over long play windows. (inferred)
- Making reactive audio/visual spectacle enhance performance clarity rather than distract from timing decisions. (inferred)
- Designing co-op synchronization and recovery systems that create shared flow instead of frustration under mistakes/latency. (inferred)

### 6. Strategic Pillars

- Rhythm you can feel: crisp cadence, readable windows, tactile feedback.
- Movement as expression: meaningful artistry layered onto score-driven mastery.
- Living environments: world/audio response that amplifies player momentum.
- Journey to greatness: progression arc from local courts to world-stage showcases.

### 9. Definition of a Useful Idea

- Directly strengthens at least one strategic pillar and does not weaken another.
- Improves either onboarding encouragement, mastery depth, expressive identity, or long-term retention in the core loop.
- Respects locked scope (PC-first, rhythm-centered gameplay, defined mode ecosystem).
- Is testable with a clear player-behavior or experience signal in discovery (prototype, playtest, or live-data proxy). (inferred)
- Has a plausible implementation path within existing product direction, not a genre/platform pivot.
- Produces a concrete hypothesis suitable for a pm-idea issue: target user, problem, proposed change, expected outcome.
